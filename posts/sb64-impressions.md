I spent a few days at **SB64**, the mid-year session of the UNFCCC Subsidiary Bodies in Bonn. It's the quiet half of the climate calendar — no heads of state, no closing-plenary theatrics — just negotiators grinding through the unresolved text that the next COP will inherit. As someone who writes embedded software and reads technical annexes for fun, this is exactly the part of the process I find most interesting, and it rarely makes the news.

![Me at the World Conference Center in Bonn](/assets/img/jalols_photo.jpeg)

## What Bonn actually is

The June sessions don't produce headlines; they produce *brackets* — the square-bracketed phrases in draft text that mark where parties still disagree. The whole job of the week is to remove a few of them so that COP has something adoptable to vote on. The two bodies doing the work are the SBSTA (the science-and-methods side) and the SBI (the implementation side), and most of the substance happens in small, half-empty rooms rather than the big halls.

It is unglamorous and slow, and that is the point. The pace forces you to pay attention to definitions, baselines, and measurement — the boring scaffolding that decides whether a headline target means anything once it's deployed.

## The measurement problem, again

The thread I kept pulling on was the one I always come back to: how do you *measure* a commitment? A lot of Bonn was spent on the indicators for the Global Goal on Adaptation — the question of what, concretely, you count to claim a country is more resilient than it was last year. It's a deceptively hard problem. A number that's easy to report is usually easy to game, and a number that's honest is usually expensive to collect.

This is where the engineering brain itches. Half of these debates are, underneath the diplomatic language, sensor-and-data problems: what gets instrumented, who owns the readings, how you reconcile a satellite estimate with a ground measurement. The policy text is downstream of infrastructure that mostly doesn't exist yet.

## Where the code meets the negotiation

The most useful conversations I had weren't in the formal sessions at all — they were in the corridors, with people building the monitoring systems the text quietly assumes. Edge sensing, MRV (measurement, reporting, verification), the unglamorous plumbing of carbon markets under Article 6. There's a real gap between what a paragraph mandates and what a field-deployable device can actually do on a power budget, and almost nobody in the room is sitting on both sides of it.

That gap is, more or less, the reason I keep coming back to this intersection.

## Bottom line

SB64 won't be remembered for any single decision — that's not what the June sessions are for. But sitting through it made the COP-to-COP machinery legible in a way that reading the outcome documents never did. The negotiations are slow because measurement is slow, and measurement is slow because the instrumentation is hard. That's a problem I'd like to keep working on.
